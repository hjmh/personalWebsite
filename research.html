Navigational behaviors in insects

I am interested in understanding how animals move through space. I am particularly fascinated by the surprisingly complex navigational capabilities of insects, which are often (falsely, I think) refered to as “simple” animals. Many ant and bee species exhibit sophisticated orientation behaviors when foraging. Other insects such as the North American monarch butterflies or the Australian bogong moths cover thousands of miles during their seasonal migration. We still know relatively little about how these complex navigational behaviors arise.

I believe that identifying and recreating important features of natural environments in the lab, and developping behavioral paradigms that are inspired by challenges animals face in the wild will be necessary to understand the underlying neural computations. This approach will be described in a more detail in the next section. So far I have worked on different aspects of navigation in Drosophila larve, adult flies, and crickets. More about these projects.

Currently I am trying to understand how visually guided navigation can change based on past experiences in walking fruit flies. More specifically, my work aims to address questions related to the use of visual landmark cues in the context of a specific, visually guided task and how this visual information is integrated with the fly’s internal representation(s) of the environment. To this end I have developed several paradigms for conditioning a fly’s preference for different visual landmarks, with the goal of training flies to seek relief from punishment or collecting a reward at one of two landmark types. These paradigms are in principle compatible with 2-photon imaging, as the fly is head-fixed and interacts with a virtual environment.

How can we build a colaborative, inclusinve research environment?

Increasing diversity, equity and inclusion in neuroscience requires attracting diverse scientists, and retaining these scientists in an environment where they can thrive.

As a female scientist in a male-dominated field, I experience gender-based bias firsthand, and as a scientist with a chronic illness, I’m sensitized to ableist practices in academia. I found support in a women’s mentoring group at my institute. The exchange of experiences and support through a peer group is invaluable in reinforcing a sense of belonging in a workplace where oneself is in a minority. If not already in place, I would establish a similar system in future workspaces.

Past projects

I spent the last few years developing behavioral paradigms for studying visually-guided navigation in head-fixed walking flies. These paradigms were designed to be compatible with two-photon calcium imaging. At the moment I’m working on porting these paradigms to an imaging rig with the goal of combining behavioral and neurophysiological studies of navigation.

Virtual reality approach

To study the neural basis of navigational behaviors, I place fruit flies in a 2D virtual reality. These experimental virtual worlds are rather abstract at first sight, but they catpture some important features of natural enviroments:

  • 2D worlds: The animal navigates a virtual 2D space, thus it’s position and what it sees at any point in time are not strictly coupled.
  • Coupling of movement and sensory experience: The animals walking maneuvres affect what the animal's sensory experience.
  • Naturatistic stimulus statistics: Sensory stimuli are generated by the animal, which results in specific stimulus statistics.
  • Rich set of visual features: Movement in 2D generates characteristic visual stimuli such as regressive optic flow, parallax and looming stimuli.
  • Approachable objects: Landmarks in these worlds are in close proximity to the fly and can be reached. They can also be approached from multiple directions and can thus be seen from different perspectives.
