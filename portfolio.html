Navigational behaviors in insects

I am interested in understanding how animals move through space. I am particularly fascinated by the surprisingly complex navigational capabilities of insects, which are often considered to be relatively “simple” animals. Many ant and bee species, for example, exhibit sophisticated orientation behaviors when foraging and eastern North American monarch butterflies cover thousands of miles during their seasonal migration.

We still know relatively little about how complex navigational behaviors arise and I think that porting some of the complexity of the natural environment and the challenges animals face in the wild to the lab will help study their neural basis. In the next section I illustrate this approach in a little more detail.

Currently I am trying to understand how visually guided navigation can change based on past experiences in walking fruit flies. More specifically, my work aims to address questions related to the use of visual landmark cues in the context of a specific, visually guided task and how this visual information is integrated with the fly’s internal representation(s) of the environment.
To this end I have developed several paradigms for conditioning a fly’s preference for different visual landmarks, with the goal of training flies to seek relief from punishment or collecting a reward at one of two landmark types. These paradigms are in principle compatible with 2-photon imaging, as the fly is head-fixed and interacts with a virtual environment.

So far I have worked on different aspects of navigation in Drosophila larve, adult flies, and crickets. You can find out more about individual projects here.


Virtual reality approach

To study the neural basis of navigational behaviors, I place fruit flies in a 2D virtual reality. While these experimental virtual worlds might rather abstract at first sight, they catpture some important features of natural enviroments the animal could have enounterd in the wild.


The fly navigates a virtual 2D space, thus it’s position and what it sees at any point in time are not strictly coupled (as is the case in existing head-fixed conditioning paradigms).
